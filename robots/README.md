## Abstract

Deployed robotics has shifted from a capital-acquisition question to a
deployment-economics question: the dominant lifetime cost of a fielded robot is
not its bill of materials but the recurring cost of integration, service, and
end-of-life decisions, all of which interact with the local price of human labor.
This paper develops a transparent, fully parameterized cost model expressing robot
total cost of ownership (TCO) as the sum of manufactured cost, deployment and
integration, lifetime service and maintenance (modeled as a multiple of manufactured
cost), operating cost, and a refurbish-or-retire decision rule, and compares it
against properly loaded human labor cost across seven wage regions and three skill
cohorts. The two senses of "depreciation" — physical robot wear (~5%/yr) and
Becker–Mincer human-capital (skill) decay (~7%/yr) — are kept rigorously separate.
The analysis shows that automation favorability is strongly regional and
cohort-specific, that utilization and shift coverage dominate hardware price, and
that the dominant rational outcome is augmentation rather than wholesale displacement.

**Keywords:** deployed robotics; total cost of ownership; automation economics;
loaded labor cost; human-capital depreciation; cohort analysis; payback period;
bill of materials; break-even wage; robot penetration; regional wage comparison.
